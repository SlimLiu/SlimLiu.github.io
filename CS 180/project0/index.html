CS180 course project — exploring how distance, focal length, and perspective transform stories on screen. From everyday selfies to cinematic illusions.

Close vs. Step-Back & Zoom

Take a close selfie → distorted. Now step back several feet, zoom in, and match head size. The second portrait should look better. Why?

Answer: Perspective depends on camera-to-subject distance. Close distance exaggerates near features; stepping back equalizes ray angles. The lens mainly sets framing—zooming just crops the field of view. So “back up + zoom” keeps head size but fixes geometry.

Architectural Perspective Berkeley Street — Distance vs. Compression

We repeated the selfie experiment in an urban setting on a Berkeley street: first, we stood far back and zoomed in for a telephoto view; then we walked forward along the street and took a second photo with no zoom (wide), adjusting our position so the scene fills the frame to about the same size in both shots.

What to notice

  • Tele / far (first photo): the layers of buildings, poles, and people appear flattened / compressed; distances feel shorter.
  • Wide / close (second photo): depth feels expanded; nearby elements grow quickly while distant ones shrink; lines diverge more.

Why this happens (theory)

In the pinhole model the on-sensor size of an object of height \(h\) at distance \(d\) with focal length \(f\) is \(\;s=\dfrac{f\,h}{d}\;\). For two scene layers (near \(d_1\), far \(d_2\)), their displayed size ratio is

\[ \frac{s_1}{s_2} \;=\; \frac{f h / d_1}{f h / d_2} \;=\; \frac{d_2}{d_1}. \]

The focal length cancels in the ratio: zoom only reframes; it does not change perspective. What changes is the camera–scene distances. When you stand far away (telephoto), both \(d_1\) and \(d_2\) are large and their ratio \(d_2/d_1\) moves closer to 1, so near and far layers project to more similar sizes → the street looks compressed. When you move close (wide), \(d_1\) drops much more than \(d_2\); the ratio \(d_2/d_1\) grows, so the size contrast between layers increases → depth expands.

The same story appears in parallax: image shift is roughly proportional to camera baseline and inversely proportional to depth (disparity \( \propto \tfrac{B}{Z} \)). From far away, relative parallax between street layers is small; from up close, it is large.

Takeaway for the Berkeley scene

  • Choose distance first: back up to tame perspective (compression for tele views), or move in to emphasize depth (wide views).
  • Then set focal length: after changing distance, pick \(f\) so the main subject matches size across shots (framing), e.g. \( f_{\text{tele}} \approx f_{\text{wide}} \cdot \dfrac{d_{\text{tele}}}{d_{\text{wide}}} \).

Assignment statement (restated): With the camera far and zoomed in, the first photo of the Berkeley street will look flatter / more compressed than the second close, wide-angle photo — because increasing distance reduces the near–far size ratio and parallax, not because of zoom itself.
